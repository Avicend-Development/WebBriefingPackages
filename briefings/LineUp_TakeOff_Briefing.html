Lesson Front Page

A

Duration

Briefing: 10 minutes
This briefing covers line-up procedures, the standard departure briefing, and normal take-off technique through to initial climb. The briefing is designed for delivery on the run-up area or ground school prior to the sortie.

B

Student Prerequisites

  • Completed BEFORE TAKE-OFF and LINE-UP checklists (SOP §1.10)
  • Familiar with aircraft attitude and trim concept (DGH 00/01)
  • Basic taxiing and RWY entry awareness
  • Understands nose attitude vs. horizon relationship
  • Has studied departure briefing format from SOP §1.9.1
C

Lesson Objectives

KNOWLEDGE
  • State the purpose of the departure briefing and its mandatory elements
  • Explain the effect of engine torque on directional control during roll
  • Identify key V-speeds: VR, VX, VY for the P-Mentor
  • Describe the emergency action for engine failure before and after VR
SKILL
  • Perform LINE-UP checklist correctly as Read & Do on run-up area
  • Deliver a complete departure briefing per SOP §1.9.1
  • Execute a normal take-off: smooth power application, rudder coordination, VR call-out, flap retraction at 400 ft AGL
  • Maintain centreline tracking throughout the take-off roll
ATTITUDE
  • Apply SOP discipline: never enter RWY without clearance and final scan
  • Demonstrate awareness of wake turbulence and situational readiness before reporting ready
  • Think in aircraft manoeuvre, not control input
  • Maintain positive control and confidence during power application
D

Tolerances

HEADING ±15°
AIRSPEED +10 / −5 kt
CENTRELINE ±4 m
ALTITUDE ±100 ft

Source: ATP INT Lesson Prep (DGH series). Tolerances to be relaxed for first exposures; tighten progressively through the lesson series.

E

Motivation

The take-off is the first phase of every flight where the student must simultaneously manage power, directional control, attitude, airspeed, and situational awareness. A disciplined line-up and departure briefing habit established early is the foundation for safe operations throughout the PPL course and beyond. Getting it right now builds the SOP muscle memory that will protect the student at every level of training.

F

Common Mistakes

  • Entering the RWY without clearance or omitting final scan
  • Abrupt or partial power application at start of roll
  • Insufficient right rudder causing yaw left during acceleration
  • Rotating too early or pulling the aircraft off prematurely
  • Failing to call out "53 — Rotate" at VR
  • Not calling "Safe speed — Flaps up" and "Max continuous power" at 400 ft AGL
  • Allowing nose to drop when selecting flaps up (nose must be raised)
  • Drifting off extended centreline during initial climb
  • Skipping or incorrectly delivering the departure briefing
  • Not considering wake turbulence before reporting ready for departure

Sources: CAPA SOP §1.11 Common Errors; ATP INT DGH series; CASA FIM

01
Briefing Section
Environment
Theory

Aerodrome Awareness

  • Identify active runway, circuit direction, and any active restricted areas prior to line-up
  • On a controlled aerodrome (e.g. EKRK), ATC clearance is mandatory before entering the runway. Do not enter if in any doubt — request confirmation
  • On an uncontrolled aerodrome, the crew must manoeuvre the aircraft sufficiently to scan at least the base leg and final approach sector before entering
  • Intersection take-offs reduce available runway — state available distance in your call-out

Source: CAPA SOP §1.10

Theory

Wake Turbulence

  • Wingtip vortices are generated by all aircraft in flight. Heavier aircraft produce stronger vortices
  • Vortices sink and drift downwind — they are a hazard on take-off especially in calm or light crosswind conditions
  • Start a timer when a medium or heavy aircraft rotates. For the Twin Otter (to be treated as medium): wait ≥ 1 min 30 sec before reporting ready for departure
  • TWR will not issue wake turbulence advisories for the Twin Otter — crew responsibility
  • Advise TWR before line-up if additional spacing is needed

Source: CAPA SOP §1.10

Theory

Threat & Error Management (TEM) — Departure Environment

  • Threats: Other traffic on or approaching the runway; wake turbulence from preceding departures; runway surface condition; low-level turbulence or gusts affecting VR and initial climb speed
  • Error traps: Rushing checklist completion; assuming clearance was given; not scanning final before entering the runway; failing to brief TEM items in the departure briefing
  • TEM mitigations: Always complete LINE-UP checklist Read & Do before entering runway; deliver departure briefing including emergency actions; confirm mental readiness before reporting ready for departure
  • If low-level turbulence or gusts are expected: brief modified take-off — delay VR by 5 kt (or ½ of gust value), climb at VY + 5 kt with flaps 1 until clear of turbulent layer

Sources: CAPA SOP §1.9.1, §1.11.1 NOTE; EGAST GA-2 Decision Making

02
Briefing Section
Setup
SOP

The BEFORE TAKE-OFF checklist must always be performed as READ & DO. The LINE-UP checklist is then completed on the run-up area before entering the runway. Do not read any checklist below 1,000 ft AGL unless circumstances require. (CAPA SOP §1.5, §1.10)

SOP Reference

Ready for Line-Up Sequence

  • BEFORE TAKE-OFF checklist: completed as Read & Do
  • LINE-UP checklist: completed on run-up area (Read & Do)
  • Verify Pitch Trim Selector set for LH (pilot flying / student side) — incorrect setting prevents pitch trim adjustment
  • HDG bug set to wind-corrected heading (WCH) to track extended centreline
  • Altitude warning set for planned level-off altitude (e.g. 1,300 ft)
  • Mental review of departure profile and emergency actions
  • Only report ready for departure when mentally prepared and all paperwork completed

Source: CAPA SOP §1.10

Departure Briefing Format

Mandatory Briefing Elements (SOP §1.9.1)

  • Take-off type — e.g. "Normal take-off, RWY 21, Roskilde"
  • Profile — turn altitude, heading, climbing altitude
  • HDG bug & altitude warning — confirm set
  • Navaids — "Navaids are set" (if applicable)
  • Emergency — before VR: "WE STOP — close throttle, apply brakes, advise ATC"
  • Emergency — after VR below 650 ft: "Nose down, 70 KIAS, throttle idle, flaps as required, land straight ahead within 45°, preferably left (or into wind)"
  • Emergency — above 650 ft: "Standard emergency procedures"
  • TEM items — if relevant (gusts, obstacles, low-level turbulence)

Source: CAPA SOP §1.9.1; IFR version §2.6

Theory

Example Departure Briefing — P-Mentor VFR (EKRK RWY 21)

"VFR departure RWY 21, Roskilde. Normal take-off. After departure climb straight ahead 650 ft, expect right turn inbound Borup HDG 235°, climbing altitude 1,300 ft. HDG bug set for WCH and altitude warning set.

In case of any failure before VR — WE STOP, close throttle, apply sufficient brakes, advise ATC.
In case of engine failure after VR and below 650 ft — nose down, speed 70 KIAS, throttle idle, flaps as required, land straight ahead within 45°, preferably to the left.
Above 650 ft — standard emergency procedures."

Source: CAPA SOP §1.9.1 — modify direction per wind, local conditions, and obstacles. Altitudes in MSL or AGL (not mixed). Always include TEM if relevant.

Theory — Aerodynamics

Forces During Take-Off Roll

  • Torque / P-factor: Engine torque and propeller slipstream cause the nose to yaw left during the take-off roll. Right rudder corrects this tendency
  • Lift build-up: As airspeed increases, lift increases proportionally to V². The aircraft becomes unstuck progressively — do not force it off the ground early
  • Ground effect: Within ~half wingspan of the runway, induced drag is reduced — the aircraft may "float" in ground effect before accelerating to VY
  • Trim change at flap retraction: Selecting flaps up removes the nose-down pitching moment — the nose MUST be raised simultaneously to maintain the climb attitude

Source: CAPA SOP §1.11.1 NOTE; CASA FIM; The FI Manual

Instructor Note — Common Errors

Watch for These During Execution

  • Abrupt power application causing torque-induced yaw before student can correct
  • Premature rotation — pulling the aircraft off before VR; stall risk in ground effect
  • Nose drop on flap retraction — watch for this at 400 ft AGL specifically
  • Drifting off centreline during initial climb — student switches visual attention to instruments too early; reinforce outside reference
  • Missing or mumbling the "Safe speed — Flaps up" / "Max continuous power" call-outs
  • Not trimming after power / flap changes — high control load leads to imprecise flying

Sources: CAPA SOP §1.11 Common Errors; ATP INT DGH series; CASA FIM

Crosswind & Turbulence Variations (SOP §1.11.2)

Modified Technique When Required

  • Crosswind (XWC > 5 kt): Hold aileron into wind throughout roll. Maintain track with rudder. Accelerate to VR + 5–10 kt, rotate more quickly than normal. Immediately allow nose to weathervane into wind to establish crab angle; level wings in the crab. Check max crosswind component not exceeded.
  • Low-level turbulence / gusts: Delay VR by 5 kt (or ½ of gust increment). Initial climb at 70 KIAS flaps 1; after flaps up, climb at VY + 5 kt (max addition 10 kt). Verify climb performance still meets obstacle clearance requirements. Brief modification in the departure briefing: omit "Normal take-off" and brief explicitly.

Source: CAPA SOP §1.11.2 and §1.11.1 NOTE

04
Briefing Section
Check

Use the following items to debrief the student and verify understanding at the end of the briefing. The FI should ask these verbally — the student should be able to answer without reference to notes before the lesson is considered ready to fly.

Knowledge Check

Oral Questions — Procedures

What checklist is completed on the run-up area, and how must it be performed?
What are the mandatory elements of a departure briefing?
What call-out is made before entering the runway? When can you omit it?
What is VR on the P-Mentor? What call-out accompanies it?
What two actions are completed at 400 ft AGL, and why must the nose be raised when selecting flaps up?
What is the emergency action if the engine fails before VR? After VR but below 650 ft?
Knowledge Check

Oral Questions — Theory

Why does the nose yaw left during the take-off roll, and how is it corrected?
What is the difference between VX and VY? When would you use VX?
How does a crosswind affect the take-off technique? Describe the aileron and rudder inputs.
Why do we wait before reporting ready for departure after the Twin Otter departs?
Why is the Pitch Trim Selector position critical before entering the runway?
What speed does the student climb at above 1,000 ft AGL, and what checklist is completed at that point?
FI Assessment Criteria

Standard to be Demonstrated Before Progressing

MINIMUM STANDARD

  • Delivers a complete departure briefing
  • Scans final and calls out before entering runway
  • Maintains approximate centreline during roll
  • Rotates at or near VR with call-out
  • Selects flaps up at 400 ft with call-out

PROGRESSION STANDARD

  • Smooth power application, no abrupt inputs
  • Rudder coordination throughout the roll
  • Nose raised on flap retraction without prompting
  • Tracks extended centreline in climb
  • All call-outs accurate and timely

CONSOLIDATION STANDARD

  • Performs all of the above without prompting
  • Maintains tolerances per ATP INT DGH series
  • Correctly modifies technique for crosswind / gusts
  • Can describe emergency procedures without reference
  • Demonstrates SOP awareness and crew resource discipline

Sources: CAPA SOP §1.10–§1.12; ATP INT DGH series tolerances; CASA FIM instructional guidance
