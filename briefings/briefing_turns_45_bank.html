Lesson Objectives

  • Perform coordinated 360° turns at exactly 45° angle of bank
  • Maintain altitude, heading accuracy and coordination throughout
  • Demonstrate controlled entry and roll-out using visual references
  • Recognise and manage overbanking tendency with aileron pressure
  • Apply appropriate power increase and back-pressure in the turn
  • Roll out within tolerances on a pre-selected heading or feature

Student Prerequisites

  • Competent in medium (30°) level turns through 360°
  • Demonstrated coordinated flight — ball discipline
  • Attitude flying: nose on horizon, not control input
  • Lookout habit established before every turn
  • Familiar with ACSEL check procedure
  • Basic understanding of forces in a turn (lift, weight, centrifugal)

Motivation

Steep turns are the foundation of advanced airwork: 720° turns, spiral recoveries, and evasive manoeuvring. Proficiency at 45° bank demands smooth, coordinated control pressure and accurate attitude flying — skills that directly transfer to all advanced flight phases. Perfect turns now save hours of remedial work later.

Common Mistakes

  • Applying aileron first, then waiting before adding back-pressure — causes a slip entry
  • Not adding power — speed decays, altitude lost
  • In left turn: not adding power immediately (Pitot on left wing causes early IAS drop)
  • Forgetting lookout before initiating the turn
  • Not starting roll-out 10°–20° before target heading
  • Failing to reduce power on roll-out — aircraft climbs as wings level
  • Over-correcting backpressure → high load factor / imminent stall
  • Attempting to raise a low nose by back-pressure alone — reduce bank first

01

ENVIRONMENT

AREA SELECTION · CONDITIONS · AIRMANSHIP

Theory — Forces in a Steep Turn

In a turn, lift must overcome both weight and centrifugal force. As bank increases, the vertical component of lift decreases — requiring increased back-pressure to maintain altitude. At 45° bank the load factor is approximately 1.41 g, raising the stall speed by ~19%. Drag increases significantly, so power must be added to maintain airspeed.

Theory — Overbanking Tendency

At 45° and above, the outside wing travels faster than the inside wing, generating more lift on the outside. This causes the bank to steepen automatically unless checked with opposite aileron pressure. This overbanking tendency distinguishes steep turns from medium turns where inherent stability holds the bank.

Theory — Load Factor & Stall Awareness

The stall speed increases proportionally to the square root of the load factor. At 45° bank: stall speed ≈ Vs × √1.41. Excessive back-pressure in a steep turn tightens the turn, further increases load factor, and can lead to an accelerated stall. Recognise the warning: buffet, reduced control effectiveness, high sink rate.

Training Area Conditions (ATP INT. Lesson Prep)

  • Good visibility and cloudbase — VMC throughout
  • Wind: non-gusty, ideally ≤15 kt for initial introduction
  • Suggested area: SW of Roskilde CTR (EKRK), 1,000–2,500 ft
  • Clear of controlled airspace, populated areas, and cloud
  • Minimum altitude for airwork: 3,000 ft AGL (or per ACSEL)

Airmanship — Collision Avoidance (EGAST GA1)

  • Collisions are most likely when aircraft are turning — maintain heightened awareness during this exercise
  • Perform clearing turns before any manoeuvre — look, then turn
  • During a turn ≈85% time on lookout, 10% attitude check, 5% instruments
  • High-wing aircraft: lift wing slightly before turning to clear the blind spot
  • Maintain continuous lookout during all 360° of the turn

02

SETUP

AIRCRAFT CONFIGURATION · ACSEL CHECK · ENTRY PREPARATION

Theory — Entry Preparation

Before every steep turn, the area must be cleared. The pilot selects a fixed visual reference point on the horizon (or sets the HDG bug) to define the entry and roll-out heading. This prevents spatial disorientation during 360° turns and anchors the awareness of when roll-out should begin.

Trim the aircraft for level cruise. Verify engine instruments are in the green. Steep turns are initiated from straight and level flight — note any lateral stability characteristics of the PA28 (inherent stability tends to resist banking above 30°).

ACSEL Check (CAPA SOP)

Call out and complete before entering the manoeuvre:

  • A — Altitude: "…ft. Manoeuvre above 3,000 ft AGL."
  • C — Configuration: "Steep turn, clean, cruise speed."
  • S — Safety: "Loose objects stowed, seat belts fastened."
  • E — Engine: "Instruments in the green, set up for airwork."
  • L — Location: "Clear of populated areas, cloud — clearing turns."

Call out: "ACSEL check completed."

Aircraft Configuration

  • Speed: normal cruise (PA28 ~100 KIAS) — do not exceed VA
  • Clean configuration — flaps up, gear up (if applicable)
  • Power: set cruise MAP/RPM (e.g. MAP 24" / 2100 RPM)
  • Trim: aircraft in trim for straight and level flight
  • Carburettor heat: as required per conditions / POH

Visual Reference Selection

  • Choose a prominent geographic feature or heading on the horizon to mark the start/end of the turn
  • Set HDG bug to current heading before entry
  • Side-by-side seating note: in a left turn, the nose appears to rise; in a right turn, it appears to lower — compensate consciously
  • Roll-out lead: begin 10°–20° before the target heading

03

EXECUTION

ENTRY · IN TURN · ROLL-OUT · INSTRUCTOR TECHNIQUE

Key principle (CAPA SOP §1.22.4): Control pressure not control movement. Aileron and backpressure must be applied simultaneously as bank increases — never wait for the bank to establish before adding elevator input.

ENTRY — 0° to 45° Bank

  1. Clear the area: look in the direction of turn before rolling.
  2. Apply aileron in the direction of turn with coordinated rudder.
  3. As bank passes 30°, simultaneously add back-pressure AND increase power to maintain speed and altitude.
    Left turn only: start adding power immediately with the roll — the Pitot tube on the left wing causes early IAS indication drop.
  4. At 45° bank: neutralise aileron and rudder inputs.
  5. Hold back-pressure to keep nose at correct position on the horizon. Cross-check: altimeter, nose position, wing angle.

IN THE TURN — Maintaining 45°

  1. Aileron: hold light opposite (top) aileron to counter overbanking tendency.
  2. Elevator: maintain constant back-pressure — nose at horizon level-turn attitude.
  3. Rudder: use for coordination — keep the ball centred.
  4. Power: maintain as set to sustain cruise speed — watch for speed decay.
  5. If nose drops too far below horizon: reduce bank first, then raise nose. Never use elevator alone to recover a low nose at 45°+.
  6. Scan: attitude → altimeter → AI → ball. 85% outside on visual references.

ROLL-OUT — 45° to Wings Level

  1. Begin roll-out 10°–20° before the target heading / visual reference.
  2. Apply opposite aileron and rudder to roll wings level.
  3. Simultaneously ease off back-pressure as bank decreases.
  4. Reduce power as bank passes through 30° on roll-out (left turn: reduce power simultaneously with roll-out start).
  5. As wings level: neutralise all control inputs and trim for straight and level.
  6. Cross-check altimeter for level flight. Call out heading achieved.

Instructor Technique Notes

  • Demo first with "talk-through" narration; student flies from second attempt
  • Introduce right and left turns — note different sight pictures in side-by-side seating
  • If time permits, progress to 720° turns once 360° is consistent
  • Cover AI/DG on later practice attempts to reinforce visual attitude flying
  • Correct overbanking tendency comment: raise it only once it becomes a habitual problem — early mention causes over-correction
  • Typical altitude profile error: diving entry then climbing roll-out — brief the student to apply backpressure co-currently with bank input

04

CHECK

ASSESSMENT · DEBRIEF · STANDARDS

Lesson Standard — Pass/Progress Criteria

The student demonstrates 360° turns at 45° bank, both left and right, meeting all four tolerances simultaneously (±100 ft, ±5° bank, ±10° heading, ±5 kt airspeed) for a minimum of one complete revolution in each direction. Coordination is maintained (ball centred) throughout entry, turn and roll-out.

Lookout & ACSEL

Clearing turns completed. ACSEL check called out correctly. Area confirmed clear before entry.

Entry Coordination

Aileron and back-pressure applied simultaneously as bank increases. No slip/skid during entry. Power added correctly (immediate in left turn).

Bank Accuracy

45° ±5° maintained throughout the turn. Overbanking tendency managed with light aileron input. No excessive aileron correction.

Altitude Control

Altitude held within ±100 ft. No significant altitude gain on roll-out. Nose attitude correctly referenced to horizon.

Coordination (Ball)

Ball centred throughout entry, turn and roll-out. Rudder used correctly for balance — not for direction. No skids or slips.

Roll-out Accuracy

Roll-out commenced 10°–20° before target. Wings level within ±10° of entry heading. Back-pressure released progressively. Power reduced on roll-out.

Debrief Points — Typical Issues

  • Slip on entry: delayed backpressure — brief "simultaneous aileron and elevator"
  • Altitude loss: insufficient power or back-pressure — review forces in turn diagram
  • Nose too low: student attempts elevator alone — reinforce "reduce bank, then raise nose"
  • Heading overshoot: late roll-out — practise half-bank lead rule
  • Speed decay in left turn: Pitot effect — must add power immediately on left roll
  • Climbing roll-out: failed to reduce power — emphasise power reduction on roll-out

Progression & Next Lesson

  • If 360° achieved at standard → progress to 720° turns
  • Link to stall awareness: demonstrate accelerated stall entry from steep turn (ACSEL required) to reinforce load factor awareness
  • Collision avoidance discussion: turning flight is highest-risk phase for mid-air collision (EGAST GA1) — reinforce lookout habit
  • Future exercises building on steep turns: steep spirals, unusual attitude recovery
