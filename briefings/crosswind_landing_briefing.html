💡 Motivation — Why It Matters

Crosswind landings represent one of the most common causes of runway excursions and hard landings in general aviation. In Denmark, winds rarely align perfectly with the runway — the ability to land safely in a crosswind is a core PPL competency and a condition of solo release at CAPA. Without correct technique, the aircraft will drift off centreline, touch down with side-load on the undercarriage, or weathercock uncontrollably after touchdown. The CAPA de-crab method (crabbed approach → simultaneous rudder/aileron in the flare) is safer and more reliable for student pilots than a pure wing-down approach. Mastering this technique enables the student to operate from any runway in variable Danish weather conditions.

⚠ Common Mistakes
  • Forgetting wind correction on downwind & base — drifting from the rectangular pattern
  • Positioning controls mechanically during final instead of flying the aircraft
  • De-crabbing too early — drift resumes before flare
  • Insufficient aileron into wind at touchdown — aircraft drifts downwind
  • Too much rudder relative to bank — sideslip turns into a forward slip
  • Removing crosswind inputs after touchdown — risk of sideways skid
  • Hesitating on the go-around decision when approach is unstabilised
  • Forgetting carburettor heat OFF during go-around ATP DGH 07

Environment
Weather, Wind Assessment & Pre-Flight Considerations
Theory — Wind & Drift

A crosswind component creates drift — the aircraft's flight path diverges from its heading. On approach, an uncorrected crosswind pushes the aircraft laterally off the extended centreline. As airspeed decreases during the flare, a given crosswind produces a progressively larger drift effect. Near the ground, the wind gradient may reduce the effective crosswind slightly, but reducing airspeed compensates — the pilot must maintain correction right through to touchdown and rollout.

Gusts add an unpredictable element: add ½ the gust value (up to max. +10 kt.) to approach and threshold speeds.

In turbulence or windshear risk: add 5 kt. to all speeds. SOP §1.18.1
Theory — Flap Consideration

In strong crosswind conditions the AFM specifies adding 5 kt. to approach and threshold speeds. Consider reduced flap settings in very strong crosswinds — some aircraft manufacturers permit or recommend flapless or partial-flap approaches. Always verify AFM limitations for the specific aircraft. The P-Mentor SOP does not restrict flap settings in crosswind beyond speed additions, however the FI must ensure the student is aware of this principle.

Maximum demonstrated crosswind component — refer to current AFM. Do not exceed limits. SOP §1.18.5
Manoeuvre — Wind Assessment

Before entering the circuit, assess:

  • Windsock orientation and estimated surface wind speed/direction
  • Crosswind component relative to RWY in use
  • Gust information from ATIS/tower and calculate speed additions
  • Identify which side the wind is from — this determines aileron/rudder direction in the flare
Overfly the windsock at 1500 ft. AGL if joining at an uncontrolled aerodrome. SOP §1.17

Setup
Circuit Geometry, Configuration & Speed Targets
Theory — Circuit Correction

The circuit must be flown as a rectangle relative to the ground, not the aircraft heading. Wind correction (crab angle) is required on every leg to track the correct ground path:

  • Crosswind leg: angle into wind to track 90° to runway
  • Downwind: apply crab angle to track parallel to RWY, 1000 ft. AGL
  • Base: allow for increased or reduced groundspeed due to wind component
  • Final: maintain crabbed heading to track extended RWY centreline

Turning base and final, the turn radius will differ depending on wind direction. An early or late base turn may be required. CASA FIM p.44

Manoeuvre — Circuit Setup Sequence

Enter circuit per normal SOP. At circuit entry establish:

  • Set 2250 RPM, MAP 22–23", 90 KIAS before circuit entry
  • Fly downwind at 1000 ft. AGL with wind correction applied throughout
  • Abeam threshold: MAP 12", speed check, select Flaps 1 — call out: "Speed XX kts. Checked — Flaps 1"
  • Reduce to 80 KIAS, commence descent
  • Base turn: check final clear — call: "Final clear"
  • Final turn complete NLT 500 ft. AGL
  • On final: select Flaps 2, propeller full forward — call: "Final check: Propeller fine — Flaps 2 set"
  • Continue at 70 KIAS; bleed to 63 KIAS at threshold (+ gust correction if applicable)

Execution
The De-Crab Technique — Final, Flare, Touchdown & Rollout
Theory — The De-Crab Method (CAPA SOP)

CAPA uses the "de-crab during flare" technique. The aircraft is flown on final in a crabbed attitude — maintaining a heading offset into wind so the ground track follows the extended RWY centreline. The nose points into wind; the flight path is straight down final. This is maintained throughout the approach.

Upon entering the flare, when just before touchdown, simultaneously: apply rudder to align the nose pointing straight down the RWY, and aileron into wind to maintain centreline. The two inputs counteract each other, resulting in a slightly lower upwind wing — the aircraft touches down on the upwind main wheel first, then the downwind main wheel, then nosewheel.

Example: Wind from the right → left rudder to align nose + right aileron to prevent drift left → touch down on right main wheel first. SOP §1.18.5

Key feedback rule: drifting right of centreline = add more bank (aileron); nose pointing right = less rudder. Inputs must match the actual conditions — fly the aircraft, don't position the controls mechanically. ATP DGH 07 / DGH 12
Theory — Sideslip vs. Forward Slip

In the de-crab method, once the rudder aligns the nose with the runway and aileron holds centreline, the aircraft is in a sideslip — heading parallel to the runway, flight path down the centreline. This is correct.

A forward slip occurs if excessive rudder turns the nose past the runway direction. This is a common student error and will cause drift off centreline. The tape-on-cowling training aid helps students recognise nose alignment. FI Manual Ch.10

Manoeuvre — Touchdown & Rollout
  • Maintain crab throughout final — do not de-crab prematurely
  • Initiate flare at normal height, then begin de-crab: simultaneous rudder + upwind aileron
  • Throttle to idle during flare — not before threshold
  • Upwind main wheel touches first — maintain aileron and rudder
  • Lower nosewheel to the ground promptly for steering authority
  • Maintain crosswind control inputs throughout the landing roll
  • Progressive opposite rudder (and brake if needed) to counteract weathercock
  • Do not remove aileron input after touchdown
High crosswind: flare less than normal to maintain positive control before touchdown. SOP §1.18.5
Go-Around Decision

The student is responsible for making the go-around decision. If the approach becomes unstabilised, centeline is significantly lost, or control is uncertain — go around without hesitation. Once initiated, a go-around must not be abandoned.

  • Call out: "Going around — Full power"
  • Full power (propeller → throttle), rotate to G/A attitude
  • Verify Flaps 1 or less; positive ROC
  • Retract flaps at safe speed (above VX 56 KIAS)
  • Climb at VY 70 KIAS; max continuous power at 400 ft. AGL
  • Carburettor heat — OFF during go-around
SOP §1.20
Touch and Go in Crosswind

After a crosswind touchdown, once rolling safely on centreline:

  • Select Flaps 1, call: "Flaps set for take-off"
  • Re-trim; verify propeller full forward
  • Apply full power — follow normal take-off procedure
  • Maintain centreline using rudder during the take-off roll
Only perform T&G if landing was stable and centreline was maintained. Go around if in doubt — never continue a T&G from a bad landing. SOP §1.19

Check
Assessment Criteria, Debrief Focus & Release Standard
Assessment — FI Observation Points
  • Wind correction applied on all circuit legs — not only final
  • Stabilised approach maintained on centreline at correct speed
  • De-crab timing: not too early, not too late — in the flare
  • Rudder and aileron inputs are simultaneous and proportionate
  • Upwind wheel touches first; no sideways drift at touchdown
  • Crosswind inputs maintained through rollout — no weathercock
  • Go-around initiated promptly when appropriate — no hesitation
  • All SOP call-outs performed correctly in sequence
Release Check Standard (DGH 12)

The crosswind landings performed in DGH 12 constitute a release check for crosswind up to 10 kt. The student must demonstrate:

  • Consistent centreline tracking throughout the circuit
  • Steady, appropriately-sized bank angle at touchdown — not large or unsteady
  • Correct technique repeated over multiple circuits
  • Independent decision-making and command authority
  • All tolerances met: altitude ±150 ft, heading ±15°, speed +10/−5 kt.
A steady and small bank angle is better than a large and unsteady one. ATP DGH 12
Debrief Key Points

After the flight, review the following with the student:

  • Was wind correction applied on every circuit leg (downwind, base, final)?
  • Was the de-crab transition smooth — neither early nor rushed?
  • Did the aircraft track centreline from final through to stopping?
  • Were rudder and aileron balanced correctly (sideslip, not forward slip)?
  • How was the weathercock tendency managed in the rollout?
  • Were go-around decisions timely and executed correctly?
  • Were all SOP call-outs completed in correct sequence?
Summary — De-Crab Method Quick Reference
DOWNWIND
Apply crab — fly rectangular pattern parallel to RWY

Maintain crab — heading into wind, track down centreline
